# I. States of Matter

## 1.1 Fundamental Principles of the Three States

The essential differences between solids, liquids, and gases stem from variations in **inter-particle distance, arrangement, and freedom of motion**.

- **Solid**: Particles are closely packed in a regular lattice structure, only vibrating in fixed positions.
- **Liquid**: Particles are slightly further apart, able to move and slide past each other, but still held by relatively strong attractive forces.
- **Gas**: Particles are far apart relative to their own size, moving freely and rapidly, colliding with container walls to produce **pressure**.

## 1.2 Energy Nature of State Changes

State changes fundamentally involve the conversion between **thermal energy** and **particle kinetic/potential energy**:

- **Melting** and **Boiling**: Solid → Liquid, Liquid → Gas. Energy must be absorbed to overcome **inter-particle attractive forces**.
- **Condensing** and **Freezing**: Gas → Liquid, Liquid → Solid. Thermal energy is released, particle kinetic energy decreases, and attractive forces pull particles closer together.

**Key interpretation of heating/cooling curves**: The plateau (constant temperature) corresponds to a state change. During this phase, all heat energy is used to change **potential energy** between particles, not **kinetic energy**.

## 1.3 Relationship Between Gas Volume, Temperature, and Pressure

According to **Kinetic Particle Theory**, when temperature increases, the average kinetic energy of particles increases, leading to more frequent and forceful collisions with container walls. If volume is fixed, pressure increases; if pressure is fixed (e.g., free expansion), volume increases.

The fundamental relationship (basis of the ideal gas law):

$$PV = nRT$$

Where:
- $P$ = Pressure
- $V$ = Volume
- $n$ = Amount of substance (number of moles)
- $R$ = Gas constant
- $T$ = Absolute temperature (unit: K)

Relationship between temperature and average particle kinetic energy:

$$\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}kT$$

Where $k$ is the Boltzmann constant and $\bar{E}_k$ is the average kinetic energy per particle.

## 1.4 Diffusion

Diffusion is the spontaneous movement of particles from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration, driven by a **concentration gradient**.

**Graham's Law of Diffusion** — The rate of diffusion is inversely proportional to the square root of the **relative molecular mass**:

$$\text{Rate of diffusion} \propto \sqrt{\frac{1}{M_r}}$$

That is:

$$\frac{\text{Rate}_1}{\text{Rate}_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_{r2}}{M_{r1}}}$$

This principle arises because, at the same temperature, lighter molecules have higher average speeds.

# II. Atoms, Elements and Compounds

## 2.1 Atomic Structure

An atom consists of a **nucleus** (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by **electron shells**:

| Particle | Relative Mass | Relative Charge |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Proton | $1$ | $+1$ |
| Neutron | $1$ | $0$ |
| Electron | $\frac{1}{1840}$ (negligible) | $-1$ |

**Atomic Number / Proton Number ($Z$)**: The number of protons in the nucleus, which determines the element's identity.

**Mass Number / Nucleon Number ($A$)**: The sum of protons and neutrons.

$$A = Z + N$$

Where $N$ = number of neutrons.

Nuclear symbol notation:

$$^{\,A}_{Z}\text{X}$$

## 2.2 Electronic Configuration

Electrons are arranged in **shells**, filling from the innermost outward, following the **2, 8, 8...** rule.

Key patterns:
- **Group number** = Number of outer shell electrons (Groups I-VII)
- **Period number** = Number of occupied electron shells
- **Group VIII (Noble gases)**: Full outer shell (He has 2, others have 8) — extremely stable

## 2.3 Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the **same element** that have the **same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons**.

$$\text{Isotopes}: \quad ^{12}_{6}\text{C},\; ^{13}_{6}\text{C},\; ^{14}_{6}\text{C}$$

**Same chemical properties** because chemical properties are determined by electronic configuration, which is identical for isotopes of the same element.

**Different physical properties** because of different masses, affecting properties such as diffusion rate and density.

## 2.4 Calculating Relative Atomic Mass

Relative atomic mass is the **weighted average** of isotopic masses (relative to $\frac{1}{12}$ of a $^{12}\text{C}$ atom):

$$A_r = \frac{\sum (\text{isotopic mass} \times \text{relative abundance})}{100}$$

Or equivalently:

$$A_r = \frac{\sum (m_i \times a_i)}{\sum a_i}$$

Where $m_i$ is the isotopic mass and $a_i$ is the percentage abundance.

# III. Stoichiometry

## 3.1 Relative Atomic Mass and Relative Molecular Mass

**Relative Atomic Mass ($A_r$)**: The average mass of one atom of an element divided by $\frac{1}{12}$ of the mass of one atom of $^{12}\text{C}$. Dimensionless.

$$A_r = \frac{\text{average mass of one atom of the element}}{\frac{1}{12} \times \text{mass of one atom of }^{12}\text{C}}$$

**Relative Molecular Mass ($M_r$)**: The sum of the $A_r$ values of all atoms in a molecule. For ionic compounds, **Relative Formula Mass** is used.

$$M_r = \sum A_r(\text{each atom in the molecule})$$

## 3.2 The Mole and Avogadro Constant

The **mole (mol)** is the SI unit for amount of substance. Definition: 1 mol of any substance contains exactly $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ elementary entities (atoms, ions, molecules, etc.). This number is the **Avogadro Constant ($L$ or $N_A$)**.

$$N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}$$

Core formula:

$$n = \frac{m}{M}$$

Where:
- $n$ = amount of substance (mol)
- $m$ = mass (g)
- $M$ = molar mass (g/mol), numerically equal to $A_r$ or $M_r$

Calculation of number of particles:

$$N = n \times N_A$$

## 3.3 Molar Gas Volume

At **room temperature and pressure (r.t.p.)** (25°C, 101 kPa / 1 atm), 1 mol of any gas occupies a volume of $24 \text{ dm}^3$.

$$V = n \times 24 \text{ dm}^3\text{mol}^{-1} \quad (\text{at r.t.p.})$$

## 3.4 Solution Concentration

Expression of concentration:

$$c = \frac{n}{V}$$

Where:
- $c$ = concentration (mol/dm³ or mol/L)
- $n$ = amount of solute (mol)
- $V$ = volume of solution (dm³)

**Unit conversion**: $1 \text{ dm}^3 = 1000 \text{ cm}^3$. To convert $\text{cm}^3$ to $\text{dm}^3$, divide by 1000.

Can also be expressed in g/dm³:

$$\text{Concentration (g/dm}^3\text{)} = \text{Concentration (mol/dm}^3\text{)} \times \text{Molar Mass (g/mol)}$$

## 3.5 Titration Calculations

For the reaction: $aA + bB \rightarrow \text{products}$

At the endpoint:

$$\frac{n_A}{a} = \frac{n_B}{b}$$

That is:

$$\frac{c_A V_A}{a} = \frac{c_B V_B}{b}$$

## 3.6 Empirical Formula and Molecular Formula

**Empirical Formula**: The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.

**Molecular Formula**: The actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule of the compound.

$$n = \frac{M_r(\text{molecular formula})}{M_r(\text{empirical formula})}$$

## 3.7 Yield and Purity

**Theoretical Yield**: The maximum amount of product predicted by the chemical equation.

**Actual Yield**: The amount of product actually obtained from the experiment.

**Percentage Yield**:

$$\text{Percentage Yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\%$$

**Percentage Purity**:

$$\text{Percentage Purity} = \frac{\text{mass of pure substance}}{\text{total mass of sample}} \times 100\%$$

**Percentage Composition by Mass**:

$$\% \text{ of element} = \frac{\text{total } A_r \text{ of element in formula}}{M_r \text{ of compound}} \times 100\%$$

# IV. Electrochemistry

## 4.1 Electrolysis

**Electrolysis**: The **decomposition** of an ionic compound (when molten or in aqueous solution) by passing an electric current through it.

Key terminology:
- **Anode**: Positive electrode (+), where **oxidation** occurs (loss of electrons)
- **Cathode**: Negative electrode (-), where **reduction** occurs (gain of electrons)
- **Electrolyte**: The molten or aqueous substance that conducts electricity and undergoes electrolysis

**Charge transfer pathway**:
1. External circuit: electrons flow from anode to cathode
2. Inside the electrolyte: cations ($+$) move toward the cathode, anions ($-$) move toward the anode

## 4.2 Predicting Products

**Molten binary compounds**:
- At the cathode: Metal cations gain electrons → metal (or hydrogen)
- At the anode: Non-metal anions lose electrons → non-metal

**Half-equations (Ionic half-reactions)**:

Cathode (reduction): $$\text{M}^{n+} + n e^- \rightarrow \text{M}$$

Anode (oxidation): $$\text{X}^{m-} \rightarrow \text{X} + m e^-$$

## 4.3 Electroplating

Using electrolysis to coat one metal onto the surface of another to improve **appearance** and **corrosion resistance**.

- Object to be plated serves as the **cathode**
- The plating metal serves as the **anode**
- The electrolyte is a soluble salt of the plating metal

## 4.4 Hydrogen-Oxygen Fuel Cells

A hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell converts chemical energy directly into **electrical energy**, with **water** as the only chemical product.

$$\text{Overall reaction:} \quad 2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Anode reaction (oxidation)**:

$$2\text{H}_2 \rightarrow 4\text{H}^+ + 4e^-$$

**Cathode reaction (reduction)**:

$$\text{O}_2 + 4\text{H}^+ + 4e^- \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

Compared to gasoline/petrol engines, fuel cells offer advantages: **zero pollutant emissions**, higher efficiency; disadvantages: **hydrogen storage and transport are difficult**, higher cost.

# V. Chemical Energetics

## 5.1 Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

**Exothermic Reaction**: **Releases** thermal energy to the surroundings, causing an increase in the temperature of the surroundings. $\Delta H$ is **negative**.

**Endothermic Reaction**: **Absorbs** thermal energy from the surroundings, causing a decrease in the temperature of the surroundings. $\Delta H$ is **positive**.

**Enthalpy Change ($\Delta H$)**:

$$\Delta H = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}}$$

- If $\Delta H < 0$: exothermic reaction
- If $\Delta H > 0$: endothermic reaction

## 5.2 Activation Energy ($E_a$)

Activation energy is the **minimum energy** that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur. Only collisions with kinetic energy $\geq E_a$ can lead to a successful reaction.

## 5.3 Bond Energy and Enthalpy Change

**Bond breaking** is endothermic (requires energy), **bond making** is exothermic (releases energy).

The net enthalpy change of a reaction:

$$\Delta H = \sum (\text{bond energies of bonds broken}) - \sum (\text{bond energies of bonds formed})$$

That is:

$$\Delta H = \sum E(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum E(\text{bonds formed})$$

- If total energy required to break bonds > total energy released by forming bonds → $\Delta H > 0$ (endothermic)
- If total energy required to break bonds < total energy released by forming bonds → $\Delta H < 0$ (exothermic)

# VI. Chemical Reactions

## 6.1 Rate of Reaction

### Collision Theory

Three requirements for a reaction to occur:
1. Particles must **collide**
2. Collisions must have the **correct orientation**
3. Colliding particles must have kinetic energy $\geq E_a$

### Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

**Concentration**: Higher concentration → more particles per unit volume → higher collision frequency → faster rate

**Pressure (gases)**: Higher pressure → smaller gas volume → higher particle density → higher collision frequency → faster rate

**Temperature**: Higher temperature → higher average particle kinetic energy → more particles with energy $\geq E_a$ → higher proportion of effective collisions → much faster rate (approximately doubles every 10°C rise)

**Surface Area (solids)**: Larger surface area → more exposed particles → higher collision frequency → faster rate

**Catalyst**: Lowers the **activation energy ($E_a$)** → more particles have energy $\geq E_a$ → faster rate. The catalyst is **unchanged** at the end of the reaction.

## 6.2 Reversible Reactions and Chemical Equilibrium

Reversible reactions are represented by the symbol $\rightleftharpoons$, indicating the reaction can proceed in both forward and reverse directions simultaneously.

### Conditions for Dynamic Equilibrium (Closed System)

When the **forward reaction rate = reverse reaction rate** and the **concentrations of reactants and products remain constant**, the system has reached **dynamic equilibrium**.

### Le Chatelier's Principle

If a condition (temperature, pressure, concentration) of a system at equilibrium is changed, the system will shift in the direction that **opposes the change**.

**Temperature change**:
- Heating → equilibrium shifts in the **endothermic** direction
- Cooling → equilibrium shifts in the **exothermic** direction

**Pressure change** (applies to gaseous reactions):
- Increasing pressure → equilibrium shifts toward the side with **fewer gas molecules**
- Decreasing pressure → equilibrium shifts toward the side with **more gas molecules**

**Concentration change**:
- Increasing concentration of a substance → equilibrium shifts to **consume that substance**
- Decreasing concentration of a substance → equilibrium shifts to **produce that substance**

**Catalyst**: Speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally, **does not change the equilibrium position**, only helps the system reach equilibrium faster.

### The Haber Process

$$ \text{N}_2(g) + 3\text{H}_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3(g) \quad \Delta H = -92 \text{ kJ/mol (exothermic)} $$

Typical conditions: 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst

### The Contact Process

$$ 2\text{SO}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{SO}_3(g) \quad \Delta H = -196 \text{ kJ/mol (exothermic)} $$

Typical conditions: 450°C, 2 atm, vanadium(V) oxide ($\text{V}_2\text{O}_5$) catalyst

## 6.3 Redox Reactions and Oxidation Numbers

### Three Levels of Definition

**1. Oxygen gain/loss definition (Core)**:
- **Oxidation**: Gain of oxygen
- **Reduction**: Loss of oxygen

**2. Electron transfer definition (Supplement)**:
- **Oxidation**: Loss of electrons
- **Reduction**: Gain of electrons

**3. Oxidation number change definition (Supplement)**:
- **Oxidation**: Increase in oxidation number
- **Reduction**: Decrease in oxidation number

### Rules for Oxidation Numbers

1. Oxidation number of an element in its uncombined state = $0$
2. Oxidation number of a monatomic ion = charge on the ion
3. Sum of oxidation numbers in a compound = $0$
4. Sum of oxidation numbers in a polyatomic ion = charge on the ion

**Oxidising Agent**: Oxidises another substance while being reduced itself (its oxidation number decreases, it gains electrons).

**Reducing Agent**: Reduces another substance while being oxidised itself (its oxidation number increases, it loses electrons).

# VII. Acids, Bases and Salts

## 7.1 Acid-Base Theory

**Arrhenius Definition (Core)**:
- Aqueous solutions of acids contain $\text{H}^+$ ions
- Aqueous solutions of alkalis (soluble bases) contain $\text{OH}^-$ ions

**Brønsted-Lowry Proton Theory (Supplement)**:
- An **acid** is a **proton donor**
- A **base** is a **proton acceptor**

**The essence of neutralisation**:

$$\text{H}^+(aq) + \text{OH}^-(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$

## 7.2 Strong and Weak Acids

**Strong Acid**: **Completely dissociated** in aqueous solution, indicated by $\rightarrow$.

$$\text{HCl}(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq)$$

**Weak Acid**: **Partially dissociated** in aqueous solution, indicated by $\rightleftharpoons$.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}(aq) \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-(aq)$$

## 7.3 The pH Scale

pH measures the **concentration of hydrogen ions**:

$$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\text{H}^+]$$

Where $[\text{H}^+]$ is in mol/dm³.

- pH < 7: Acidic
- pH = 7: Neutral
- pH > 7: Alkaline

## 7.4 Classification of Oxides

- **Acidic Oxides**: e.g., $\text{CO}_2, \text{SO}_2$, correspond to non-metals, react with bases to form salt and water
- **Basic Oxides**: e.g., $\text{CuO}, \text{CaO}$, correspond to metals, react with acids to form salt and water
- **Amphoteric Oxides**: e.g., $\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3, \text{ZnO}$, react with both acids and bases to form salt and water

## 7.5 Preparation of Salts

**Soluble salts** preparation methods:
- Acid + alkali (by titration)
- Acid + excess metal
- Acid + excess insoluble base
- Acid + excess insoluble carbonate

**Insoluble salts** preparation: **Precipitation**

### General Solubility Rules

- Sodium, potassium, and ammonium salts: all soluble
- Nitrates: all soluble
- Chlorides: soluble (except $\text{AgCl}, \text{PbCl}_2$)
- Sulfates: soluble (except $\text{BaSO}_4, \text{CaSO}_4, \text{PbSO}_4$)
- Carbonates: insoluble (except $\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3, \text{K}_2\text{CO}_3, (\text{NH}_4)_2\text{CO}_3$)
- Hydroxides: insoluble (except $\text{NaOH}, \text{KOH}, \text{NH}_4\text{OH}$, and $\text{Ca(OH)}_2$ partially soluble)

## 7.6 Water of Crystallisation

**Hydrated**: A substance that is chemically combined with water molecules, e.g., $\text{CuSO}_4 \cdot 5\text{H}_2\text{O}$

**Anhydrous**: A substance containing no water

# VIII. The Periodic Table

## 8.1 Structure and Principles of the Periodic Table

Elements are arranged in **periods** and **groups** in order of **increasing atomic (proton) number**.

**Across a Period (left to right)**: **Metallic character decreases**, **non-metallic character increases**.

**Down a Group**: Elements in the same group have the **same number of outer shell electrons**, therefore **similar chemical properties**.

- Groups I-VII: The group number = number of outer shell electrons = common charge of the ion formed
- The number of occupied electron shells = the period number

## 8.2 Group I — The Alkali Metals

Trends down the group: **Melting point decreases, density increases, reactivity increases**

Why reactivity increases: Atomic radius increases, the outer electron is further from the nucleus and less tightly held, making it easier to lose.

## 8.3 Group VII — The Halogens

Trends down the group: **Density increases, reactivity decreases, colour deepens**

- Fluorine: pale yellow gas
- Chlorine: pale yellow-green gas
- Bromine: red-brown liquid
- Iodine: grey-black solid

**Displacement reactions**: A more reactive halogen will displace a less reactive halogen from its compound.

$$\text{Cl}_2 + 2\text{Br}^- \rightarrow 2\text{Cl}^- + \text{Br}_2$$

Why reactivity decreases: Atomic radius increases, making it harder to gain an additional electron.

## 8.4 Transition Elements

**Characteristics**:
1. High density
2. High melting points
3. **Form coloured compounds**
4. Often act as **catalysts**
5. **Variable oxidation states** (e.g., $\text{Fe}^{2+}$ and $\text{Fe}^{3+}$)

## 8.5 Noble Gases (Group VIII)

**Full outer electron shell** (He: 2; Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn: 8), therefore **extremely unreactive**, existing as **monatomic gases**.

# IX. Metals

## 9.1 Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonding is the **electrostatic attraction** between **positive metal ions** and a **"sea" of delocalised electrons**.

Explanation of metal properties:
- **Electrical conductivity**: Delocalised electrons can move freely
- **Malleability and ductility**: Layers of metal ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond (the delocalised electrons continue to hold the structure together)

## 9.2 Reactivity Series

In order of decreasing reactivity:

$$\text{K} > \text{Na} > \text{Ca} > \text{Mg} > \text{Al} > \text{C} > \text{Zn} > \text{Fe} > \text{H} > \text{Cu} > \text{Ag} > \text{Au}$$

Reactivity reflects the **tendency to form positive ions**. More reactive metals lose electrons more readily to form cations.

## 9.3 Alloys

Alloys are **mixtures** of a metal with other elements. Because the different-sized atoms disrupt the regular arrangement of metal layers, **the layers can no longer slide over each other easily**, making alloys **harder and stronger** than pure metals.

## 9.4 Corrosion of Metals

**Rusting** of iron requires both **oxygen** and **water**.

$$\text{Rust} = \text{hydrated iron(III) oxide} \quad \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \cdot x\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Prevention methods**:
- **Barrier methods** (painting, greasing, coating with plastic): Exclude oxygen and water
- **Galvanising** (zinc coating): Acts as both a barrier and provides **sacrificial protection**
- Principle of sacrificial protection: Zinc is more reactive than iron, so it preferentially loses electrons (is oxidised), protecting the iron

## 9.5 Extraction of Metals

The extraction method depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series:
- Low reactivity metals (e.g., $\text{Cu, Ag, Au}$): can be obtained directly from their ores
- Medium reactivity metals (e.g., $\text{Zn, Fe}$): **reduction with carbon** (e.g., blast furnace for iron)
- High reactivity metals (e.g., $\text{Al}$): **electrolysis**

### Blast Furnace (Extraction of Iron)

Core reduction reaction:

$$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{CO} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe} + 3\text{CO}_2$$

Slag formation (removal of $\text{SiO}_2$ impurity):

$$\text{CaO} + \text{SiO}_2 \rightarrow \text{CaSiO}_3$$

### Extraction of Aluminium (Electrolysis)

Bauxite ($\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$) is dissolved in molten cryolite ($\text{Na}_3\text{AlF}_6$) to lower the melting point.

Cathode reaction:

$$\text{Al}^{3+} + 3e^- \rightarrow \text{Al}(l)$$

Anode reaction:

$$2\text{O}^{2-} \rightarrow \text{O}_2 + 4e^-$$

The carbon anodes react with the oxygen produced, forming $\text{CO}_2$, so they must be **regularly replaced**.

# X. Chemistry of the Environment

## 10.1 Testing for Water and Purity

**Tests for water**:
- Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate (white) turns blue with water: $\text{CuSO}_4 + 5\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{CuSO}_4 \cdot 5\text{H}_2\text{O}$
- Anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride (blue) turns pink with water: $\text{CoCl}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{CoCl}_2 \cdot 6\text{H}_2\text{O}$

**Purity test**: Pure water melts at 0°C and boils at 100°C. Impurities alter these physical constants.

## 10.2 Composition of Air and Air Pollutants

**Composition of clean, dry air**:
- Nitrogen ($\text{N}_2$): ~78%
- Oxygen ($\text{O}_2$): ~21%
- Noble gases + carbon dioxide: ~1%

**Major air pollutants and their sources**:

- $\text{CO}_2$: Complete combustion of carbon-containing fuels → **global warming**
- $\text{CO}$: Incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels → **toxic gas**
- Particulates: Incomplete combustion → **respiratory problems**
- $\text{CH}_4$: Decomposition of vegetation, animal digestion → **global warming**
- $\text{NO}_x$: Car engines (high temperature) → **acid rain, photochemical smog**
- $\text{SO}_2$: Combustion of fossil fuels containing sulfur compounds → **acid rain**

## 10.3 The Greenhouse Effect

Greenhouse gases ($\text{CO}_2, \text{CH}_4$) **absorb and reflect** the **thermal radiation** emitted from the Earth's surface, reducing heat loss to space and causing global warming.

## 10.4 Photosynthesis

$$\text{6CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 + 6\text{O}_2 \quad (\text{chlorophyll, light energy})$$

# XI. Organic Chemistry

## 11.1 Homologous Series

A homologous series is a family of compounds with the **same functional group** and the **same general formula**, where each successive member differs by a $-\text{CH}_2-$ unit. Physical properties show a gradual trend, and chemical properties are similar.

**The four key homologous series and their general formulae**:

| Series | General Formula | Functional Group | Bond Type |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Alkanes | $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+2}$ | None (C-C single bonds) | Saturated |
| Alkenes | $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n}$ | $\text{C}=\text{C}$ double bond | Unsaturated |
| Alcohols | $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+1}\text{OH}$ | $-\text{OH}$ (hydroxyl group) | — |
| Carboxylic acids | $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+1}\text{COOH}$ | $-\text{COOH}$ (carboxyl group) | — |

**Saturated vs Unsaturated**:
- Saturated: All C-C bonds are **single bonds**
- Unsaturated: Contains one or more C-C **double bonds** (or triple bonds)

## 11.2 Structural Isomers

Structural isomers are compounds with the **same molecular formula but different structural formulae**.

Example: $\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$ has two isomers:
- Butane: $\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CH}_2\text{CH}_3$
- 2-methylpropane: $\text{CH}_3\text{CH}(\text{CH}_3)\text{CH}_3$

## 11.3 Fractional Distillation of Petroleum

Petroleum (crude oil) is a **mixture** of hydrocarbons, separated by **fractional distillation** based on differences in boiling point.

In the fractionating column:
- Bottom: Long-chain hydrocarbons (high boiling point, high viscosity)
- Top: Short-chain hydrocarbons (low boiling point, high volatility, low viscosity)

## 11.4 Substitution Reactions of Alkanes

Alkanes are generally unreactive, but under **ultraviolet light**, they undergo **substitution reactions** with chlorine:

$$\text{CH}_4 + \text{Cl}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{UV}} \text{CH}_3\text{Cl} + \text{HCl}$$

A substitution reaction is one in which one atom or group of atoms is **replaced** by another atom or group of atoms.

## 11.5 Addition Reactions of Alkenes

Alkenes, due to their C=C double bond, undergo **addition reactions**. The double bond opens, and atoms are added to each carbon, forming **only one product**.

**Reaction with bromine/bromine water** (used to test for unsaturation):

$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_4 + \text{Br}_2 \rightarrow \text{C}_2\text{H}_4\text{Br}_2 \quad (\text{red-brown bromine water decolourises})$$

**Addition of hydrogen** (with Ni catalyst):

$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_4 + \text{H}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Ni}} \text{C}_2\text{H}_6$$

**Hydration** (with acid catalyst):

$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_4 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{acid}} \text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}$$

### Cracking

Long-chain alkanes are broken down into shorter-chain alkanes and alkenes (and hydrogen) at **high temperature** with a **catalyst**.

$$\text{C}_{16}\text{H}_{34} \rightarrow \text{C}_8\text{H}_{18} + \text{C}_8\text{H}_{16}$$

## 11.6 Production of Ethanol

**Fermentation**: Glucose is converted to ethanol by yeast at 25-35°C in the absence of oxygen:

$$\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 \rightarrow 2\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH} + 2\text{CO}_2$$

**Hydration of ethene**: Ethene reacts with steam under pressure with an acid catalyst.

## 11.7 Carboxylic Acids and Esterification

A **carboxylic acid** reacts with an **alcohol** in the presence of an acid catalyst to form an **ester** and water:

$$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{H}^+} \text{CH}_3\text{COOC}_2\text{H}_5 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

This is an **esterification reaction**, which is a type of **condensation reaction**.

## 11.8 Polymers

### Addition Polymerisation

Monomers containing a C=C double bond open up and join together to form a long chain.

Example: Poly(ethene): $n$ monomers of $\text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2$ → $[-\text{CH}_2-\text{CH}_2-]_n$

### Condensation Polymerisation

Monomers react together, and each bond formation is accompanied by the elimination of a **small molecule** (such as water).

**Polyamides**: Dicarboxylic acid + diamine → forms amide linkages $-\text{CO}-\text{NH}-$

**Polyesters**: Dicarboxylic acid + diol → forms ester linkages $-\text{CO}-\text{O}-$

### Proteins

Proteins are **natural polyamides**, formed from **amino acid** monomers through condensation polymerisation.

General structure of an amino acid:

$$\text{H}_2\text{N}-\text{CHR}-\text{COOH}$$

Where R represents different types of side chains.

# XII. Experimental Techniques and Chemical Analysis

## 12.1 Chromatography

**$R_f$ value (Retention Factor)**:

$$R_f = \frac{\text{distance travelled by substance}}{\text{distance travelled by solvent front}}$$

The $R_f$ value is characteristic of a substance under specific conditions and is used for **identification of unknown substances**.

## 12.2 Identification of Ions

### Anion Tests

| Ion | Reagent | Observation |
|------|---------|-------------|
| $\text{CO}_3^{2-}$ | Dilute acid | Produces $\text{CO}_2$ (turns limewater milky) |
| $\text{Cl}^-, \text{Br}^-, \text{I}^-$ | Dilute $\text{HNO}_3$ + $\text{AgNO}_3$ | Precipitate forms (white/cream/yellow) |
| $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$ | Dilute $\text{HNO}_3$ + $\text{Ba}(\text{NO}_3)_2$ | White $\text{BaSO}_4$ precipitate |
| $\text{NO}_3^-$ | Al foil + $\text{NaOH}$ | Produces $\text{NH}_3$ |

### Cation Tests

Using $\text{NaOH}$ or $\text{NH}_3$ solution produces characteristic coloured hydroxide precipitates (e.g., $\text{Cu}^{2+}$ blue, $\text{Fe}^{3+}$ reddish-brown, $\text{Fe}^{2+}$ green).

### Flame Tests

Different metal ions produce characteristic flame colours:
- $\text{Li}^+$: Crimson red
- $\text{Na}^+$: Yellow/orange
- $\text{K}^+$: Lilac (viewed through a cobalt glass)
- $\text{Ca}^{2+}$: Brick red
- $\text{Ba}^{2+}$: Apple green
- $\text{Cu}^{2+}$: Blue-green

## 12.3 Identification of Gases

| Gas | Test | Positive Result |
|-----|------|-----------------|
| $\text{NH}_3$ | Damp red litmus paper | Turns blue |
| $\text{CO}_2$ | Bubble through limewater | Turns milky/cloudy |
| $\text{Cl}_2$ | Damp litmus paper | Turns red then bleaches white |
| $\text{H}_2$ | Lighted splint | "Pop" sound (squeaky pop) |
| $\text{O}_2$ | Glowing splint | Relights |
| $\text{SO}_2$ | Acidified $\text{KMnO}_4$ solution | Purple colour decolourises |
